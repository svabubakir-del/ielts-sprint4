The creative brain

Scientists have shown that creativity is not simply a product of high intelligence. Research suggests that beyond a certain point, a higher IQ does not automatically improve creative ability — in fact, a person with a moderate IQ can sometimes be far more creative than someone with exceptional intelligence. One key feature of highly creative individuals is that they possess less latent inhibition, the brain's natural tendency to block out unnecessary background stimuli. As a result, creative people absorb more sensory information than others, giving them a larger pool of material from which to form original ideas. Studies have also shown that the creative process involves two separate stages: a quiet, relaxed phase and a more active, organised phase of cortical arousal. Creative individuals switch between these two states naturally, without great effort. Interestingly, social connections also matter — many highly creative people depend on at least one trusted relationship to support and sustain their work.
