Transcription

You will hear a number of different recordings, and you will have to answer questions on what you hear. There will be time for you to read the instructions and questions, and you will have a chance to check your work. All the recordings will be played once only.

The test is in four parts. Write all your answers in the listening question booklet. At the end of the test, you will be given ten minutes to transfer your answers to an answer sheet.

PART 4

You will hear an ecologist giving a presentation on research on a type of snail. First, you have some time to look at questions 31 to 40. Now listen carefully and answer questions 31 to 40.

Good afternoon. The topic I've chosen for my presentation is a snail — the white-lipped grove snail.

The snail gets its name from the fact that it has a white edge, or lip, to its shell. Its principal source of nutrition is plants, which are either already dead or in the process of rotting. [→ Q31: plants]

A final point about its ecology is that it also needs calcium and other minerals to keep it healthy. To get these, it frequently consumes soil, which contains these minerals. [→ Q32: soil]

These white-lipped snails appeared to live in two strikingly separate areas: Ireland and the Pyrenees. But what is most surprising is that there was no record of the variety being found anywhere in between.

Via the Internet, they set up what is known as a megalab, recruiting numerous volunteers across Europe to be responsible for the collection of snail specimens, which were then sent to the university lab. [→ Q33: collection]

The second theory proposed is called natural migration, which would necessitate the snails swimming the sea to Ireland. This was ruled out because they hate salt, and simply wouldn't have embarked on the journey across the sea. [→ Q34: salt]

The team then started reviewing findings from various locations in the Pyrenees, which showed that mounds of white-lipped snail shells had been thrown away in caves. Clearly these had been inhabited by Stone Age communities. [→ Q35: caves]

This led to the conclusion that the snails must have been used over a long period by these Stone Age humans as food, and, interestingly, this was before the advent of agriculture. [→ Q36: food]

The team then looked at other types of archaeological evidence from prehistoric times in Ireland and the Pyrenees, and they found evidence that very early on there had been trade between the people living in these widely separated areas. [→ Q37: trade]

The research team have tentatively concluded that the most likely way in which the snails travelled from east to west was in boats, and that they were transported live to keep them fresh for consumption on the journey. [→ Q38: boats]

The research team recognise that they need to look at a broader sample of the snails. [→ Q39: sample]

They have found that there is probably at least one other type of organism which shows the same distribution pattern. This is a species of tree which they want to look at in more detail. [→ Q40: tree]

That is the end of Part 4. You now have one minute to check your answers to Part 4.
