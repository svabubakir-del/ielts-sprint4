Climate and human migration

Modern understanding of early human migration patterns has been significantly shaped by palaeoclimatological research. Scientists now know that the African continent experienced repeated cycles of dramatic environmental change over the past 200,000 years, alternating between arid periods — during which vast deserts expanded and human populations were confined to isolated refuges — and humid phases, during which rainfall increased dramatically and lush vegetation spread across previously barren regions. These humid periods, known as African Humid Periods, created fertile corridors that allowed human populations to expand rapidly into new territories. Genetic research supports this model, showing that patterns of genetic diversity in modern human populations correspond closely to the timing of these climatic cycles. The most significant dispersal event, which saw human populations spread rapidly into Asia, Europe, and eventually the Americas and Australia, appears to have coincided with a major shift in the Earth's orbital cycle approximately 70,000 years ago.

Out of Africa: The Scientific Consensus on Human Origins

For decades, anthropologists debated whether modern humans originated in a single region of Africa or emerged simultaneously in multiple locations across the globe. The multiregional hypothesis, once supported by a number of influential researchers, proposed that populations of Homo erectus who had already colonised Europe and Asia gradually evolved into modern humans independently, with sustained gene flow between regions preventing excessive divergence. This model was largely dismantled by genetic evidence in the 1980s and 1990s, which demonstrated conclusively that all living humans share a common maternal ancestor — popularly known as "Mitochondrial Eve" — who lived in Africa approximately 150,000 to 200,000 years ago.

Today, the scientific consensus firmly supports the "Out of Africa" hypothesis, also known as the Recent African Origin (RAO) model. According to this framework, anatomically modern humans — Homo sapiens — evolved on the African continent and subsequently expanded outward in a series of dispersal events, the most significant of which began approximately 70,000 years ago. This expansion was likely triggered by dramatic environmental changes, including volcanic supereruptions and the resulting global cooling that reduced habitable land across the tropics.

Archaeological evidence supports this trajectory. Stone artefacts, personal ornaments, and fossilised human remains found at coastal sites in South Africa, Ethiopia, and Morocco predate any comparable finds outside the continent by tens of thousands of years. As these populations colonised new territories, they encountered and in some cases interbred with archaic human relatives such as Neanderthals and Denisovans, leaving genetic traces that persist in modern non-African populations today.

The study of this extraordinary migration is necessarily multidisciplinary, drawing on genetics, geology, palaeoclimatology, and archaeology to reconstruct the complex journey by which a small population of African ancestors gave rise to all eight billion people alive today.

Passage A — Fossil Evidence and Dating

The study of early human fossils has been transformed by advances in radiometric dating techniques. Before the development of methods such as uranium-series dating and electron spin resonance, archaeologists relied almost entirely on stratigraphic position — the principle that objects found deeper in the soil are older — to assign approximate dates to hominin remains. Modern dating techniques have since revealed that many sites previously assumed to be of similar age actually span tens of thousands of years, fundamentally altering our understanding of the timeline of human evolution. In particular, fossils from sites in Morocco, such as those found at Jebel Irhoud, have been dated to approximately 300,000 years ago — pushing back the known age of anatomically modern Homo sapiens by more than 100,000 years.

Passage B — Tool Technology and Cognitive Development

The relationship between toolmaking and cognitive development in early humans has been a subject of intense debate. The Oldowan tool tradition, which produced simple stone flakes and choppers, dates back approximately 2.6 million years and is associated with Homo habilis — the first member of the genus Homo. By contrast, the later Acheulean tradition, characterised by large, symmetrical handaxes, required considerably more planning and spatial reasoning, suggesting a marked increase in cognitive capacity. The most dramatic cognitive leap, however, appears to be associated with the Middle Stone Age, when Homo sapiens began producing microlith-based composite tools, engraving abstract patterns onto ochre, and trading shell ornaments across distances of hundreds of kilometres — behaviours that imply a sophisticated symbolic communication system almost certainly linked to the development of complex language.

Passage 1 — Climate and Migration Corridors

Palaeoclimatic data indicates that major episodes of human migration out of Africa corresponded closely with periods of increased rainfall and the formation of "green corridors" — vegetated pathways across otherwise inhospitable desert regions such as the Sahara and the Arabian Peninsula. During humid phases, these corridors allowed populations to move northward and eastward with relative ease, following seasonal water sources and the grazing animals that depended on them. When dry conditions returned and the corridors closed, isolated populations were left to adapt to their new environments independently, driving regional diversification.

Passage 2 — The First Australians

The colonisation of Australia represents one of the most remarkable feats of early human migration. Genetic and archaeological evidence now places the first arrival of modern humans on the Australian continent at between 50,000 and 65,000 years ago — a period when sea levels were lower but a significant open-water crossing of at least 90 kilometres was still required. This implies that these early colonisers possessed not only the navigational skills to undertake a planned sea voyage but also the organisational capacity to sustain a founding population large enough to survive and thrive in an entirely new continent with no prior human occupation.

Passage 3 — Genetic Diversity and the Founder Effect

One of the most striking findings of modern population genetics is that African populations exhibit far greater genetic diversity than populations on any other continent. This pattern is consistent with the Out of Africa hypothesis: since all non-African populations descend from a relatively small founding group that left Africa around 70,000 years ago, they carry only a subset of the genetic variation present in their ancestral homeland. With each successive migration — from Africa to the Middle East, from Asia to the Americas — populations became progressively less diverse, a phenomenon known as the "serial founder effect".

Passage 4 — Coastal Migration

A growing body of evidence supports the theory that early modern humans expanded out of Africa by following coastlines rather than moving through the continental interior. This "coastal migration hypothesis" is supported by the discovery of ancient shell middens — refuse heaps of discarded shellfish — at sites along the southern coast of Africa, the Arabian Peninsula, and the Indian subcontinent, all dating to the period of the initial dispersal. Coastal environments offered a reliable and diverse food supply, and the absence of large predators in intertidal zones made foraging far less dangerous than hunting in the interior savannah.
