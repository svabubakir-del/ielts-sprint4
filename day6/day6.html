Perception and the constructed mind

Unlike simple mechanical devices such as cameras, the human eye does not produce a direct recording of the world around it. Instead, the brain receives incomplete visual data and actively constructs a mental image using a process of unconscious inference — drawing logical conclusions from partial sensory input. This means that what we see is not a precise copy of reality but an interpretation shaped by our expectations and prior experiences. One consequence of this is that the brain can be deceived by optical illusions, in which it perceives something that is not physically there. The brain's habit of imposing patterns and structure on ambiguous data generally serves us well, but it can also lead to systematic errors in perception that no amount of rational thinking can easily correct.
