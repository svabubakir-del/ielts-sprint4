The olfactory pathway

The human olfactory system is considerably more complex than scientists once believed. Unlike other sensory inputs such as sight and hearing, smell signals travel directly to the brain's emotional and memory centres without first passing through the thalamus. This means that scents can trigger emotional reactions and vivid memories almost instantaneously, before the conscious mind has had time to identify the source of the smell. Neuroscientists now recognise that the olfactory bulb — the structure that receives signals from receptor cells in the nasal cavity — has direct connections to the amygdala and the hippocampus, the regions responsible for emotional processing and memory consolidation respectively. Research has also shown that the sense of smell is not fixed; regular practice in identifying and categorising scents can lead to measurable changes in the physical structure of the brain, demonstrating a remarkable capacity for neuroplasticity.
