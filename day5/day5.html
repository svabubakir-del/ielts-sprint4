The health cost of early farming

The transition from foraging to farming is often described as one of the most important developments in human history. However, recent archaeological discoveries suggest that early farmers were not healthier than their hunter-gatherer predecessors — in fact, the opposite was often true. Analysis of ancient skeletons reveals that early agricultural communities suffered from higher rates of malnutrition, dental decay, and repetitive joint injuries. The primary reason for this was dietary monotony: while foragers consumed a wide variety of wild foods, farmers depended heavily on a single staple crop. This made early farming communities highly vulnerable to famine whenever harvests failed due to drought, flood, or disease. Despite these disadvantages, farming communities grew rapidly because a surplus of food allowed populations to increase, which foraging alone could never support.

Decide True or False for each statement.
