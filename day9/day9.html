Sleep and memory consolidation

The relationship between sleep and memory has been one of the most intensively studied areas of neuroscience over the past three decades. Scientists now know that sleep plays an active role in memory consolidation — the process by which temporary memories are stabilised and transferred into long-term storage. Without adequate sleep, this consolidation process is disrupted, leading to measurable deficits in recall and learning. Research has also shown that sleep protects memories from interference: while we are awake, new sensory experiences constantly compete with recently formed memories, threatening to overwrite them. During sleep, however, the brain is shielded from incoming sensory data, allowing it to replay and reinforce the day's most important information. Studies of patients with sleep impairment have revealed that even a single night of poor sleep can result in significant cognitive decline the following day.
