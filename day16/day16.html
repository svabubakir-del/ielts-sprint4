Light, hormones, and seasonal depression

Seasonal affective disorder (SAD) is a form of depression that follows a seasonal pattern, typically beginning in autumn and lasting through winter. It is closely linked to reduced exposure to natural sunlight, which disrupts the body's production of melatonin — a hormone that regulates sleep and wakefulness. Research has shown that the brain's master clock, the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN), depends on morning light signals to maintain a healthy hormonal cycle. When these signals are absent, as in winter at high latitudes, melatonin can leak into daytime hours, causing chronic fatigue and low mood. Light therapy — using specially designed lamps that simulate natural daylight — has emerged as one of the most effective treatments for SAD. Studies have found that regular morning sessions with a high-intensity light box can significantly reduce symptoms in the majority of sufferers. However, scientists have also found that cultural attitude plays an important role: populations that view winter positively tend to report lower rates of winter depression, regardless of the amount of sunlight they receive.
