Human evolution and migration

The study of human evolution has been transformed in recent decades by advances in both genetics and archaeological science. Modern researchers now adopt a multidisciplinary approach, combining evidence from fossil remains, ancient artefacts, and DNA analysis to reconstruct the trajectory of human migration across the globe. It is now widely accepted that our earliest ancestors originated in Africa and gradually began to colonise other continents around 70,000 years ago, adapting to radically different habitats as they expanded into new territories. The occupation of diverse environments — from tropical forests to Arctic tundra — required early humans to diversify their diets, tools, and social structures. This remarkable capacity to adapt is widely regarded as intrinsic to the evolutionary success of our species. The fossil record, though incomplete, provides sustained evidence of this diversification, offering insights into how early human populations survived and thrived across an increasingly habitable planet.
