The science of reading the past

Modern archaeology relies on a variety of scientific tools to reconstruct ancient civilisations. One of the most important of these is radiocarbon dating, which measures the rate of decay of carbon particles in organic material to establish its age. This technique was developed in the 1940s and transformed the study of prehistoric human remains. Before its invention, archaeologists had to depend on indirect methods such as pottery typology — comparing the style and design of ceramic fragments to establish which historical period they belonged to. Today, archaeologists also study animal bones left behind at ancient sites to determine what people ate and how they lived. Even microscopic plant fossils called phytoliths, which survive in soil for thousands of years, can reveal the diet of an ancient community. Together, these methods give scientists a remarkably detailed picture of daily life in the ancient world.
